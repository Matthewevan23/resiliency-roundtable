‚ùå Weak Prompt

"Generate Socratic seminar questions about Night by Elie Wiesel"

What AI Actually Gives You:

  1. What is the main theme of Night?
  2. How does Elie's relationship with his father change throughout the book?
  3. What role does faith play in the story?
  4. Why do you think Elie Wiesel wrote this book?
  5. What emotions did you feel while reading?
  6. How does the title "Night" relate to the book's themes?
  7. What did you learn about the Holocaust from this book?
  8. Would you recommend this book to others? Why or why not?

‚ö†Ô∏è Generic book club questions. Could apply to any Holocaust text. No scaffolding. Mixes easy and hard questions randomly. Doesn't account for 13-year-olds or personal connections.

‚úÖ Strong Prompt

CONTEXT: I'm facilitating a Socratic seminar with 13-year-olds (8th graders) who just read the first three chapters of Night by Elie Wiesel. This is their first Holocaust text. Some students are from families with Holocaust connections, others are learning about it for the first time.

TASK: Generate 8-10 questions that move from concrete/text-based to analytical to philosophical.

CONSTRAINTS: Start with questions that ground us in the text. Build to harder questions about faith, humanity, and memory. Create productive tension but honor that some students may have personal connections to this history. Questions should help students sit with difficulty, not resolve it too quickly.

What AI Actually Gives You:

Text-Based (Concrete - Ground Everyone):

  1. In Chapter 1, Moishe the Beadle returns to Sighet with a warning. What specifically does he tell people, and how do they respond?
  2. Find a moment in Chapters 1-3 where Elie describes something changing in his town. Read that passage aloud. What's happening?
  3. Elie writes, "Never shall I forget that night..." (page 34). What is he listing in this passage? Why do you think he repeats "Never shall I forget"?

Analytical (Making Meaning):

  1. People in Sighet don't believe Moishe's warning. Why not? What would it take for you to believe something that horrible?
  2. Elie's father is a community leader. How does that affect the choices their family makes - or doesn't make?
  3. Elie describes the journey from Sighet to Auschwitz. What changes happen to people during that journey - even before they arrive?

Philosophical (Sitting with Hard Questions):

  1. Elie begins questioning God in these chapters. Is it possible to be angry at God and still be Jewish? What does faith look like when the world doesn't make sense?
  2. By Chapter 3, Elie writes, "Never shall I forget those moments that murdered my God and my soul." Can a soul be murdered? What does that mean?
  3. The people in Sighet kept hoping things wouldn't get worse. When - if ever - is hope dangerous?
  4. At the end of Chapter 3, Elie is separated from his mother and sister forever. He writes about that moment but doesn't tell us his feelings. Why might he have written it that way?

‚úì Scaffolded progression. Text-specific. Creates space for personal response while keeping focus on the text. Honors the weight of the material. Age-appropriate cognitive complexity.

üí° Key Insight: The weak prompt gives you generic questions you could ask about any book. The strong prompt gives you a carefully sequenced discussion that meets 13-year-olds where they are, grounds everyone in the text first, then builds to the hard philosophical questions - while creating space for students with family connections to this history.

Reflection Prompting Mastery

Moving beyond "How did that feel?" to prompts that reveal transferable insights

The Problem

Bad reflection prompts:

  • "How did that make you feel?"
  • "What did you learn?"
  • "Did you have fun?"

Why they fail: Surface-level, generic, don't connect experience to meaning

The Solution

Great reflection prompts:

  • Create cognitive dissonance ("I thought X would happen, but Y happened instead...")
  • Use metaphor to unlock meaning ("If this experience was a season, which would it be and why?")
  • Connect to transfer ("When have you felt this same tension in your real life?")
  • Honor different processing styles (talking, writing, drawing, moving)

The Protocol: Reflection Prompt Generator

STEP 1: The Template

What educators fill in (2 minutes of notes):

[What experience did students just have?]
Example: "We just did a trust fall exercise where they had to catch each other"

[What's the core insight you want them to discover?]
Example: "Vulnerability is a prerequisite for trust, not a result of it"

[What format works for this group?]
Example: "Talking circle - they're good at speaking, resistant to writing"

STEP 2: AI Generates

What AI creates:

Opening Questions (Concrete):

  • "At what exact moment did you decide to let go and fall? What was happening in your body?"
  • "What did you notice about the people catching you before you fell?"

Deepening Questions (Analysis):

  • "I'm curious‚Äîdid you need to trust them before you fell, or did trust happen after? Explain."
  • "What had to be true for you to let go? What did you need from them?"

Transfer Questions (Application):

  • "Where in your real life are you waiting to feel safe before you're vulnerable? What if it doesn't work that way?"
  • "This week, where might you need to 'fall' before trust can happen?"

STEP 3: Educator Refines

Read through, adjust language to match your voice, add student names where helpful, pick the 4-5 questions that feel most powerful

üéØ The Power Move: AI handles the cognitive scaffolding (concrete ‚Üí analysis ‚Üí transfer). You bring the knowing of your students and the magic of your facilitation.

Quick Wins Across Teaching Domains

The same prompt framework works for all your top-voted needs. Here are three rapid examples:

üåü Culturally Responsive Curriculum (5 votes)

The Challenge: You have a great lesson on the Civil Rights Movement, but it centers Rosa Parks and MLK exclusively. You want to include local activists and lesser-known voices.

CONTEXT: I'm teaching 9th grade US History. We're studying the Civil Rights Movement. My students are mostly Latinx and Asian American in Oakland, CA.

TASK: Adapt this lesson to center Oakland's own Civil Rights history and connect it to movements students' families might have been part of (Chicano movement, Asian American activism, immigrant rights).

CONSTRAINTS: Don't erase the national narrative‚Äîshow how local and national connect. Include 3-4 primary sources (images, quotes, newspaper clips). Make it relevant to their neighborhoods today.

What AI gives you:

  • The Black Panther Party's Free Breakfast Program (started in Oakland)
  • Yuri Kochiyama's solidarity between Black and Asian American communities
  • The Third World Liberation Front strike at SF State (1968)
  • Connection to current housing justice movements in Oakland
  • Primary source: Photo of Black Panthers and Brown Berets together

üí¨ Discussion Facilitation (3 votes)

The Challenge: You're about to facilitate a philosophical chairs discussion on a hot-button topic and need questions that create productive tension without blowing up the room.

CONTEXT: Teaching 11th grade Literature. Students just read "The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas" (the town's happiness depends on one child's suffering). Half my students are very progressive, half are more conservative. The discussion could get heated.

TASK: Generate 6-8 philosophical chairs prompts that scaffold from easier to harder, from textual to philosophical.

CONSTRAINTS: Avoid prompts that let students virtue-signal. Force real moral reckoning. Balance individual ethics vs. collective good. Make sure there are legitimate reasons to stand on either side.

Sample prompts AI generates:

  • Easier: "The people of Omelas are morally responsible for the child's suffering." (Agree/Disagree)
  • Medium: "If you could end all poverty by causing one specific person to suffer in a basement, you should do it." (Forces personal stake)
  • Harder: "Your comfort right now depends on someone else's suffering, and you're okay with that." (Direct application)

üé® Differentiated Instruction (3 votes)

The Challenge: You have neurodiverse learners who need the same content delivered differently.

CONTEXT: Teaching photosynthesis to 7th graders. I have students with ADHD, autism, dyslexia, and anxiety. They need multiple entry points to the same concept.

TASK: Create 4 versions of the same lesson for different learning styles: visual/spatial, kinesthetic/movement, verbal/linguistic, and logical/sequential.

CONSTRAINTS: Same learning goal (understand how plants convert light to energy). Same time limit (20 minutes). Assessment should work for all versions.

What AI gives you:

Visual/Spatial: Diagram-building activity with color-coded arrows showing energy flow

Kinesthetic: Students become molecules‚Äîphysical movement simulation of the process

Verbal/Linguistic: Write the "autobiography" of a water molecule going through photosynthesis

Logical/Sequential: Flowchart creation with if/then decision points

  • Your expertise (knowing your students, your content, your goals)
  • + AI's speed (generating variations, finding examples, scaffolding questions)
  • + Your finishing touches (adjusting language, adding your voice, customizing)
  • = Better teaching in less time
